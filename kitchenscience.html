Kitchen Science

(A question of pressure)

Context

Goal: Arrive at a common understanding with a backyard maple syrup producer

Role: Sole Writer

Target Audience: All home cooks, but especially those at high elevations

The Challenge

Please fix your website's online Boiling Temp Calculator. The boiling point of water depends only on the atmospheric pressure at the location you are boiling. Period. However, your Boiling Point Calculator, when used per the instructions, requires inputting the atmospheric pressure as well as the altitude.

Yes, atmospheric pressure, therefore the boiling point, changes with altitude. If you need to know the boiling temp at a known altitude, for instance you plan to climb to a camping spot and boil a meal, you can get an approximate boiling point using any of a number of charts that show the boiling temperature at that altitude. However, inputting the current atmospheric pressure at your location AND altitude into your online calculator results in an incorrect lower boiling point. The higher your altitude - the more incorrect the answer.

An example of a calculator that works correctly: Boiling Point Calculator (calctool.org) Thanks, Steve

My Reply

Hi Steve - I think I understand your concern, and I hope I can provide a satisfactory explanation.

You are correct that the boiling point of water depends only on atmospheric pressure, and that altitude is simply a proxy for pressure. However, barometric pressure is generally reported by weather stations as a "corrected" value, meaning that it has been standardized to a like-sea-level value for a given location. For example, at this moment, Weather Underground is reporting barometric pressure here in American Fork as 30.02 inches, and in San Francisco as 30.16 inches Hg:

https://www.wunderground.com/forecast/us/ut/american-fork
https://www.wunderground.com/weather/us/ca/san-francisco/37.78,-122.42

The reported pressure at sea level is indeed higher than here, but not nearly higher enough to account for 4600 feet of elevation difference. And indeed, if I plug 30.02 inches Hg into the boiling point calculator you linked, the resulting boiling point is 212.17°F—which I can confirm empirically is not correct, as water here boils closer to 204°F.

This is why the boiling point calculator on our website requires values for both elevation and (corrected) barometric pressure; it is in effect "uncorrecting" the value for pressure by using the elevation information. It turns out there is at least one dedicated calculator on the web that does the uncorrection operation:

http://www.csgnetwork.com/barcorrecthcalc.html

And when I plug in 30.02 inches at 4633 feet of elevation, the uncorrected value is 25.44 inches Hg. And when I plug that into the boiling point calculator you linked, I get a resulting value of 203.8°F, which is exactly right. I hope you find this explanation congenial—but please let me know if you don't. I'll be glad to correct any mistakes I've made.

Best regards,
Adam Streed
