Cooking Technique

A customer service conversation

Project Context

Goal: Answer a deceptively complex question from a home cook

Role: Sole Writer

Target Audience: Backyard BBQ cooks

The Question

Greetings! I have your Thermopen1, SquareDot, and some other accessories I purchased from you. My main interest is smoking/BBQ, and I have an SNS Travel Kettle (18"). Hopefully you can make clear a subject that has presented itself controversial in the "BBQ Universe". Silver skin is considered connective tissue and long, slow cooking is quoted as breaking down, or melting connective tissue. Almost all BBQ pitmasters say to remove it as it won't break down, but I've heard where a few say it's okay to leave it on. Would ThermoWorks please set this straight for me once and for all? Respectfully, Theodore

My Reply

Hi Theodore - Thanks for the question! It's a great question; answering it is going to take a bit of ground-clearing. First, connective tissue in animals comes in many types, and these types have different protein compositions—some will break down with the application of low and slow cooking, but some won't. So although in a sense it's true that BBQ cooking "melts connective tissue," it's also not precise enough to settle the question about silverskin. Silverskin isn't tendons, ligaments, or collagen; it is fascia:

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fascia

Fascia is largely composed of the protein elastin, which (unlike collagen) will not break down with the application of heat. It will remain stubbornly tough, and it almost doesn't break down even with extended chewing. You're probably familiar with this specific tissue from a ribeye or rib roast, where it underlies the rib cap (spinalis) muscle; sometimes it also appears on a short plate or short rib, if the butcher hasn't already trimmed it away. When you're trimming meat with silverskin, it appears as a clearly identifiable shiny band or sheet of tough, striped tissue. Here's a photo of silverskin on a pork tenderloin:

Now a further complication here is that not everything people colloquially refer to as 'silverskin' is actually this specific type of tissue. Various membranes, such as what appears on the underside of a brisket, also get called silverskin, even though they're not the tough elastin membranes like what's pictured above. And those membranes, being composed of other proteins, can break down just fine during a BBQ cook. Some pitmasters remove them, and some don't.

Finally, to complicate matters even more, there are some connective tissues in meat which are not silverskin (in the sense of elastin-based fascia), but which don't break down during cooking, but also aren't necessarily a problem for the end product. Take the membrane that appears on the inside of the rib cage, on the underside of a rack of ribs. It doesn't break down during a BBQ cook, and many people remove it ahead of time, but it's not going to ruin your ribs if you leave it on. So the complete answer to your question is: if by 'silverskin' you mean specifically the shiny, elastin-based fascia membrane, then yes—remove it! It won't break down. But if you mean something else, then it might be just fine to leave on the meat, depending on what it is and personal taste. Is that a satisfactory answer? I hope so, but please don't hesitate to follow up with any further questions.
